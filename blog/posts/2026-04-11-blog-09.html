India's Export Renaissance: Building Tomorrow's Trade Powerhouse

India's merchandise exports hit $776 billion in 2025-26, driven by services, manufacturing, and strategic trade partnerships. Discover the opportunities ahead - by Jayesh Parab.

← All Posts

By Jayesh Parab — 11 April 2026

India's emergence as a global trade powerhouse has reached unprecedented heights in 2026, with the nation positioning itself as a critical link in the world's supply chains. As someone deeply invested in understanding global commerce patterns, Jayesh Parab has witnessed firsthand how India's strategic approach to international trade is reshaping not just our domestic economy, but our standing on the world stage.

The numbers tell a compelling story. India's merchandise exports reached $776 billion in the fiscal year 2025-26, marking a remarkable 12.8% growth from the previous year. More significantly, our services exports crossed $354 billion, cementing India's position as the world's leading services exporter. This dual-engine growth model – combining manufacturing prowess with service excellence – represents a fundamental shift in how the world perceives Indian commerce.

The New Geography of Indian Exports

What's particularly striking about India's current export trajectory is the diversification of both products and destinations. Traditional markets like the United States and European Union continue to be significant, but we're seeing explosive growth in trade with Southeast Asia, Latin America, and Africa. The UAE remains our largest trading partner, with bilateral trade exceeding $88 billion, while our trade with Vietnam has grown by 47% year-over-year.

From a sectoral perspective, India's export basket has evolved dramatically. While textiles and pharmaceuticals remain strong performers, we're seeing remarkable growth in electronics, renewable energy equipment, and high-value engineering goods. The electronics exports alone have grown to $29 billion, making India the fourth-largest electronics exporter globally.

The success in renewable energy exports is particularly noteworthy. Indian solar panels, wind turbines, and energy storage systems are now finding markets across 67 countries. This aligns perfectly with global sustainability goals while positioning India as a clean technology leader.

Services: The Crown Jewel of Indian Exports

India's services sector continues to be the crown jewel of our export economy. Information Technology and Business Process Management services account for $206 billion of our total services exports, but the real growth story lies in emerging areas. Global Capability Centers (GCCs) have become a $64 billion industry, with over 1,750 centers operating from India serving global corporations.

Professional services, including legal, accounting, and consulting, have grown by 23% annually. Indian firms are not just providing back-office support but are increasingly involved in strategic decision-making for global corporations. The digitization wave has enabled Indian professionals to serve clients across time zones seamlessly, making India the preferred destination for knowledge-intensive services.

Engineering services represent another high-growth area, with Indian companies providing design and development services for aerospace, automotive, and infrastructure projects worldwide. The sector has grown to $47 billion, with Indian engineers working on everything from spacecraft design to sustainable urban planning projects.

Manufacturing Renaissance and Make in India 2.0

The manufacturing sector's contribution to exports has been equally impressive. The Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme has transformed India into a global manufacturing hub for smartphones, pharmaceuticals, and automotive components. Apple's decision to manufacture 14% of its global iPhone production in India has been a game-changer, with Indian-made iPhones being exported to over 40 countries.

Pharmaceutical exports have reached $27.9 billion, with India supplying critical medicines to 200+ countries. The pandemic demonstrated India's capacity as the 'pharmacy of the world,' and this reputation has only strengthened. Indian generic drugs account for 20% of global generic medicine exports, providing affordable healthcare solutions worldwide.

The automotive sector tells an equally compelling story. India exports vehicles and components worth $22.5 billion annually, with companies like Tata Motors, Mahindra, and Bajaj establishing global footprints. The shift toward electric vehicles has opened new opportunities, with Indian EV manufacturers beginning to export to European and Southeast Asian markets.

Digital Infrastructure as an Export Product

One of India's most innovative export developments has been the internationalization of our digital public infrastructure. The India Stack – including Aadhaar, UPI, and DigiLocker – is being adopted by countries seeking to build robust digital ecosystems. Singapore, UAE, and several African nations have implemented Indian digital solutions, creating a new category of 'digital exports.'

The Unified Payments Interface (UPI) has been particularly successful, processing 13.2 billion transactions monthly domestically and expanding internationally. Indian fintech solutions are being exported to 23 countries, representing a $4.2 billion market opportunity.

As Jayesh Parab often emphasizes, this digital infrastructure export represents India's transition from a service provider to a solution creator, fundamentally changing how the world views Indian technological capabilities.

Trade Partnerships and Strategic Alliances

India's approach to international trade has become increasingly strategic, moving beyond bilateral agreements to comprehensive economic partnerships. The India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor (IMEEEC), announced at the G20 Summit, promises to revolutionize trade routes and reduce logistics costs by 40%.

The Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement (CEPA) with the UAE has yielded remarkable results, with bilateral trade growing 68% since its implementation. Similar agreements with Australia and Mauritius have opened new markets for Indian goods and services.

The Indo-Pacific Economic Framework provides another avenue for enhanced trade cooperation, particularly in supply chain resilience and digital trade. India's active participation in the Quad economic initiatives has strengthened our position in the critical minerals and semiconductor supply chains.

Challenges and Opportunities Ahead

Despite remarkable progress, challenges remain. Logistics costs continue to be higher than global benchmarks, though initiatives like the National Logistics Policy are addressing this systematically. The Bharatmala and Sagarmala projects are creating world-class infrastructure to support export growth.

Currency volatility affects export competitiveness, but the increasing acceptance of rupee trade settlements with multiple countries is mitigating this challenge. Russia, Iran, and several other nations now accept rupee payments, reducing forex dependency.

Skill development remains crucial for sustaining export growth. The government's Skill India mission has trained 14 million people in export-relevant skills, but scaling this further is essential for meeting global demand.

The Road to $1 Trillion in Exports

India's target of achieving $1 trillion in combined merchandise and services exports by 2030 appears increasingly achievable. The current trajectory suggests we could reach $900 billion by 2028, with the final push to $1 trillion driven by emerging sectors like space technology, biotechnology, and advanced materials.

State-level export promotion has become equally important. Gujarat, Maharashtra, and Tamil Nadu continue to lead, but states like Odisha, Telangana, and Karnataka are emerging as significant export contributors. Goa's focus on high-value pharmaceutical and IT exports has positioned the state as a quality-over-quantity player in the global market.

Reflecting on this transformation, Jayesh Parab believes that India's export success story is not just about numbers but about building sustainable competitive advantages that will serve the nation for decades. The focus on innovation, quality, and strategic partnerships positions India not just as a low-cost producer but as a preferred partner for global businesses seeking reliability, quality, and technological sophistication.

As we look toward the remainder of this decade, India's role in global trade will only expand. The combination of demographic advantages, technological capabilities, and strategic vision makes India not just a participant in global trade but a key architect of its future direction. The export renaissance we're witnessing today lays the foundation for India's emergence as a developed economy by 2047.

— Jayesh Parab, Goa
